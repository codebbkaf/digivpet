## 2026-07-17 — US-004: Add SpriteFrame enum and runtime sheet slicing — PASSES

Did:
- Sources/SpriteSheet.swift (one new file, no changes to SpriteLoader or project.yml):
  - `SpriteFrame`: Int-raw enum, walk1=0 ... attack=11. Raw value IS the sheet index, so the
    case order is the sheet layout — do not reorder.
  - `EggFrame`: idle=0, wobble=1, hatch=2 for 48x16 Digitama.

    `kind` is .stage (12 frames) or .egg (3 frames).

    (defaults to SpriteLoader.loadSheet), NSLock-guarded, `.shared` singleton.

Verified (observed, not assumed):
- `xcodebuild build` -> ** BUILD SUCCEEDED **, zero source warnings.
- `xcodebuild test` -> ** TEST SUCCEEDED **, Executed 18 tests (12 new), 0 failures.
- SpriteFrame.attack.sourceRect == (32, 48, 16, 16); walk1/eat1/eat2/hurt1 rects asserted as
  hand-read literals, not by recomputing the formula against itself.
- Child/Agumon -> kind .stage, 12 frames, each 16x16. Digitama/Agu_Digitama -> kind .egg,
  exactly 3 frames.
- Cache: a counting decoder shows 3 sheet() calls for Child/Agumon = 1 decode, and Gabumon
  decodes separately (right key). Second sheet() returns the IDENTICAL CGImage objects (===

DESIGN NOTE — US-005 depends on this:
- EggFrame and SpriteFrame are SEPARATE enums, and each subscript returns nil for the other
  kind of sheet. Egg index 2 is the HATCH but a stage sheet's index 2 is EAT1, so one shared
  enum would make sheet[.eat1] silently return the hatch — the same class of quiet-wrong-art
  bug US-002 found in Bundle.url(forResource:).
  Consequence: `sheet[SpriteFrame.walk1]` on a Digitama is nil BY DESIGN. DigimonSpriteView
  must animate eggs with EggFrame (idle -> wobble) and must NOT fall back to walk frames.
- Failed loads are cached too (cache is [Key: SpriteSheet?]), so a missing sprite does not
  re-hit the disk on every 500ms animation tick.
- Build sprite views on SpriteSheetCache.shared.sheet(stage:name:) — do not call
  SpriteLoader.loadSheet directly from a view, or you re-decode per frame.

DELIBERATE DIFFERENCE FROM US-012 — do not "unify" these two windowing rules:
- Sleep is CLIPPED to its window. US-012's daily quantities are assigned WHOLE to the day they
  START in. The rules differ because the data does: a 900-step sample never says when each step was
  taken, so splitting it would be invention, while a sleep sample's value IS its span and clipping
  it is exact.
- It matters at the 18:00 edge. Someone asleep at 17:30 would otherwise lose the whole night, since
  no other window claims it: consecutive nights do NOT overlap (last night's ends at noon, tonight's

  nap is not last night's sleep. testConsecutiveNightsDoNotOverlap pins the no-double-count half.
- The window is anchored to the local DAY, not to `now`, so it does not drift while the user
  watches. 03:00 reads the night in progress; 23:00 reads the night that ended this morning, not
  the one just beginning.
- DST is covered: the spring-forward night is 17 hours, not 18. Offsets from midnight would run
  past noon into the afternoon the window must never claim.

THE ONE DESIGN DECISION LATER STORIES MUST KNOW:
`updatedAt` moves forward by exactly the intervals APPLIED — never to `now`. Two consequences,
both deliberate and both tested:
1. A part-worn interval is CARRIED. Stamping `now` would mean two 3h sessions accrue nothing,
   and anyone who opens the app more often than every 4h would never get hungry at all.
2. At the cap nothing is applied, so the timestamp FREEZES at the instant hunger hit max.
   US-027's "hunger at max for 8h+" care mistake reads (now - hungerUpdatedAt) and needs no
   second saved date. testTheTimestampStopsAtTheInstantHungerHitTheMaximum pins this.

*** US-024 (feeding) MUST RESTAMP hungerUpdatedAt = now WHEN IT FEEDS. ***
That is the cost of point 2: without it, feeding a starving Digimon down to 3 would instantly
re-accrue every interval the stale timestamp had been sitting at max, and the feed would look
like it did nothing. Restamping on feed is the natural reading of a feed anyway.

Edge cases handled (all tested): nil timestamp (a save written before this existed) starts the
clock and adds nothing, rather than inventing elapsed hunger from birthDate and double-counting
against the hunger such a save already holds; a BACKWARDS clock restamps rather than parking
the timestamp in the future where hunger would freeze until the wall clock caught up; an absurd
elapsed time saturates instead of trapping (the cap comparison is done in Double space BEFORE
Int(), because Int(Double) traps outside Int's range and elapsed is only as sane as the device
clock).

THE FOUR RULES (all feed the single `careMistakeCount` EvolutionEngine already gates on)

- Starvation: 1 mistake per 8h at maximum hunger, charged as a RATE (24h = 3). Reads
  `hungerUpdatedAt` directly, which works ONLY because HungerClock freezes that stamp at the
  instant hunger hit the cap — no second saved date. `starvationMistakesCharged` is the marker;
  it resets to 0 the moment hunger drops off the cap, so a fed-then-starved-again Digimon does
  not have its first hour instantly charged.
- Silent day: 1 per WHOLE elapsed local day with no health data. TODAY IS NEVER CHARGED (it is
  still in progress). The marker moves forward by exactly the days CHARGED, never to today —
  same discipline as HungerClock.advance — so the day in progress is charged once, after it ends.
- 3 refusals in one day, and waking a sleeping Digimon: both capped at one mistake per day by
  their own day markers (`refusalMistakeDay`, `wakeMistakeDay`), not by the raw counts. Prodding
  a sleeping Digimon six times is one bad night, not six.

BUG #1 IN THE INHERITED CODE (fixed — this is the important one)

The audit was called with `healthDataSeen: readings.values.contains { $0.hasData }`. `hasData` is
true only for `.value`, so it lumped `.unavailable` in with `.noData` and charged a care mistake
for HEALTHKIT BEING OFF OR FAILING. `HealthReading.unavailable`'s own docstring says the opposite
in as many words ("US-027 must not charge a care mistake for a HealthKit failure the user did
nothing to cause"), and CareMistakes.swift's own parameter doc claimed it was doing that. It was
not. Replaced the Bool with `CareMistakes.HealthDataVerdict` — seen / silent / unreadable:
  - any `.value`      -> seen: stamp the marker, charge nothing.
  - else any `.noData` -> silent: HealthKit answered and had nothing. The only case charged.
  - else               -> unreadable: stamp WITHOUT charging. Not charging is obvious; the reason
    it must still STAMP is not — leaving the marker behind would make the first read that finally
    worked charge the whole week HealthKit was off.
Note `.noData` also covers DENIED (HealthKit makes denied and empty indistinguishable for reads),
so a user who denies everything does accrue mistakes. Not fixable at this layer; flagging it.

BUG #2 (fixed) — my own first draft of the silent-day model test expected 1 and got 5. Not a
flaky test: over the 2 days it spans, hunger also maxed out and 32h of starvation legitimately
charged 4 more. The fixture, not the code, was wrong.

2026-07-18 — US-028 — Add sickness state and cure

WHAT I BUILT

Sources/Sickness.swift — the thresholds (3 care mistakes make it sick, 30 energy in one local day
cures it) and `GameState.updateSickness(energyEarnedToday:)`. Nothing here derives anything from
elapsed time: US-027's audit has already turned time into mistakes by the time this runs, so
sickness is a pure function of the count and the day's energy and needs no clock or marker of its
own. Called from `refresh()` immediately after `auditCareMistakes` and before `evolveIfReady`, for
both of the same reasons the audit sits there — the mistake that tips a Digimon over is usually one
the audit has only just charged, and a sick Digimon's evolution is paused, so settling sickness
after `evolveIfReady` would let it evolve one refresh into an illness it already had.

THE ONE REAL DESIGN DECISION: THE CURE IS CHECKED BEFORE THE ILLNESS

`updateSickness` switches on the CURRENT status rather than running both checks in sequence, and
the cure case comes first. Consequence: a Digimon that crosses 3 mistakes on a day it has already
earned 30+ energy goes sick on this refresh and is cured on the NEXT one. That is deliberate. The
alternative order cures it inside the same call, so three mistakes would never once be visible on
an active day and AC1 would be unobservable in the app. Pinned by
testFallingSickOnAnActiveDayIsNotUndoneInTheSameRefresh, which asserts BOTH halves — sick now,
healthy on the following call — so nobody later "fixes" the ordering without seeing the intent.

The cure resetting careMistakeCount to 0 (AC3) is the ONLY thing in the whole game that ever
lowers that count. It is not decoration: without it a cured Digimon relapses on its very next
refresh off the same three mistakes it was just forgiven. testACuredDigimonDoesNotRelapse-
Immediately is that test. NOTE FOR US-029: this means care mistakes are no longer monotonic, so a
death rule keyed on "ever reached N" needs its own marker rather than reading the live count.

`EnergyLedger.creditedToday.total`, passed in rather than read off `GameState`. The ledger is
deliberately not part of the saved game (its own docs say why: `resetGame` would hand a reborn
Digimon the same day's steps a second time), and it is already the thing that knows what today
credited, so no new state was added for this story. `GameState` gained no new fields at all —
`healthStatus` already existed from US-006.

THE FORMULA, AND WHY EACH WEIGHT IS THAT SIZE

    power = 1 + 8 * battleRung(stage) + 3 * strengthStat + lifetimeEnergy.total / 25

- base 1 keeps power STRICTLY positive. US-031 is likely to want a ratio of two powers, and a fresh
  Digitama would otherwise be a zero to guard at every such site.
- stage at 8/rung is deliberately the dominant term — evolving is the reward the game is built on,
  and Ultimate is worth 48. This is what makes testAHigherStageAndStrengthBeatsAHoarderOfLifetime-
  Energy true: an Adult with 10 training beats a Baby I holding 400 lifetime energy.
- strengthStat at 3 makes TRAINING the fastest lever the user has. A `TrainAction` session costs 5
  energy and buys 3 power; the same 5 energy merely accumulated would buy 0.2. Spending on purpose
  should beat hoarding, and at these weights it does, 15x.
- lifetime energy at 1 per 25 is the slow floor, and it is `lifetimeEnergy` and NOT `stageEnergy`
  precisely because it is the one term that survives evolution and (via US-029's carry) death.

ARMOR-HYBRID IS THE ONE STAGE THAT NEEDED A DECISION

`Stage.ladderIndex` is nil for `.armorHybrid` by design (US-006: it is a side branch, not a rung).
A `?? 0` fallback would have made an Armored Digimon fight exactly as hard as an egg, which is the
one answer that is certainly wrong. `BattlePower.battleRung` scores it as an Adult (4), the tier it
branches toward, and testArmorHybridFightsAsAnAdult pins that so the nil is never silently defaulted
again.

2026-07-18 — US-031: Add turn-based battle resolution and animation
================================================================================

WHAT I DID

Two new files — `Sources/Battle.swift` (the rules) and `Sources/BattleView.swift` (the screen) —
plus a `battle()`/`finishBattle()` pair on `MainScreenModel`, a `BattleControls` row and a battle
overlay in `ContentView`, and `Tests/BattleTests.swift`.

THE SPLIT THAT MAKES THIS TESTABLE

The battle is RESOLVED IN FULL before a single frame is drawn. `BattleEngine.resolve` is pure, takes
its randomness `inout` from an injected generator, and returns a `BattleReport` — every exchange in
order, plus the winner. `BattleView` is then a REPLAY of a decided outcome. That is why AC6 is
assertable without a screen, and why the view needs no rules.

THE MODEL, AND WHY THE NUMBERS ARE THESE NUMBERS

    both sides start on 10 HP; damage = random(1...maximumDamage)
    maximumDamage(attacker, defender) = max(1, 8 * attacker / (attacker + defender))

- SHARED hit points, with power deciding how hard you HIT rather than how much you TAKE. Scaling HP
  with power would make a strong Digimon's battles LONGER precisely because it is strong, and the
  screen has to animate every exchange at 0.7s.
- The ceiling is the attacker's SHARE of the two powers, not its absolute power. So the RATIO is what
  matters: an even match at 10-vs-10 and one at 1000-vs-1000 roll the same range
  (testTheDamageCeilingDependsOnTheRatioNotTheMagnitude pins this). Absolute scaling would mean an
  Ultimate one-shots a Child and two Ultimates trade 40-point blows — the same battle, bigger numbers.
- minimum damage 1 is what GUARANTEES TERMINATION: a hopeless underdog still chips, so no battle can
  stall. The 40-turn cap is therefore unreachable at these constants (double the worst case) and
  exists only so a retune cannot produce an infinite loop. Its tiebreak is documented and tested.
- The player always swings first. A battle is something the user chose to start, and losing the
  initiative to an invisible coin flip reads as the game cheating.

AC5 IS THE ONE I WOULD NOT LET PASS ON INSPECTION

`GameState.recordBattle` moves `battleWins`/`battleLosses` and NOTHING else — that "nothing else" IS
the criterion. Two tests hold it: `testLosingCausesNeitherDeathNorACareMistake` asserts healthStatus,
sickSince, diedAt, careMistakeCount and hunger all unchanged, and
`testALosingStreakStillCausesNeitherDeathNorACareMistake` runs 25 straight losses, because the
single-loss version would pass on a rule that kills after five. Through the real model,
`testLosingAFoughtBattleLeavesTheDigimonAliveAndUnmarked` fights a REAL losing battle and then runs
`refresh()` — sickness and death are settled there, so a punishment deferred to the next foregrounding
would still be a punishment, and this catches it.

weaker set of rules that only runs in the background — that is how the two drift.

THE RESCHEDULE IS UNCONDITIONAL

`performRefresh()` re-arms AFTER the refresh, whether or not it succeeded. A chain that only re-armed

suspends an app that leaves one outstanding and stops granting it wakes.

AN OBSERVED UPDATE DOES NOT RESCHEDULE

`healthDataChanged()` refreshes but does NOT re-arm the timer. An observer that did would push the
next guaranteed wake half an hour further out every time the user took a walk, so a busy day would
starve the schedule of exactly the wake it exists to guarantee.

  data day = 5 mistakes, sick), because an equality assertion alone cannot catch a change that broke
  both runs identically.
- Supporting: `testTheBattleAllowanceRollsOverWhileTheAppIsShut` (US-032's rollover needs nothing to
  run at midnight) and `testTheReopenAfterAClosureStillCreditsTodaysHealthData`.

WHY THE SILENT-DATA FIXTURE IS THE ONE THE 48h TEST USES

With an empty fetcher every metric reads `noData` -> verdict `.silent`, and the silent path charges
missed days WITHOUT stamping `healthDataLastSeen = now`, so the open and shut runs charge identically.
Under a `.seen` fixture they would NOT: the open app stamps the marker every hour and charges nothing,
while the shut one charges 1 for the day it never observed. That is not a bug in the recompute — it is
data the closed app genuinely never had, and the same reason ENERGY cannot be reconstructed from
elapsed time (it is read for the day it is read on). That is precisely what the schedule buys, and
`testTheReopenAfterAClosureStillCreditsTodaysHealthData` covers the day it comes back to.

KNOWN, PINNED, NOT FIXED

`sickSince` and `diedAt` are stamped when the app NOTICES, not when the state became due — the
instant `careMistakeCount` crossed three is not saved anywhere to derive it from. So a Digimon that
fell ill during a closure starts its 72h death countdown at the reopen rather than at the hour it
became ill. It errs towards LENIENCY (the countdown starts later, never earlier), which is the right
way for it to be wrong. `ElapsedTimeState` excludes the two markers and
`testAnIllnessThatBeganWhileClosedIsDatedFromTheReopen` asserts the behaviour, so it cannot change
unnoticed. Fixing it properly means timestamping each care mistake, which is a change to US-027's
rule and does not belong in this story.

WHAT WAS BUILT

- `DigiVPetComplicationExtension` in `project.yml`: an `app-extension`, embedded via a dependency on
  the app target, `NSExtensionPointIdentifier = com.apple.widgetkit-extension`, bundle id under the
  app's own (`com.digivpet.DigiVPet.Complication`). Verified in the built product: the `.appex` is in
  `DigiVPet.app/PlugIns/`.
- `ComplicationSnapshot` + `ComplicationSnapshotStore`: the app writes a small JSON file into the
  `group.com.digivpet.DigiVPet` container at the end of EVERY `MainScreenModel.refresh()`, and the
  widget only reads it. The widget never opens a `GameStore` — a second store on the same file means
  a second in-memory `EnergyLedger` and the same steps credited twice, which is the exact bug
  `GameSession` exists to prevent. The snapshot deliberately carries no `GameState`, no `EnergyType`
  and no graph, so the extension links neither SwiftData nor the evolution model.
- `ComplicationViews.swift` (compiled into BOTH targets) holds the provider and the two family views;
  `Sources/Complication/DigiVPetComplication.swift` holds only the `Widget` and the `@main`
  `WidgetBundle`, and `Sources` excludes `Complication/**` from the app so there is one entry point.
- Sprites are bundled a SECOND time into the `.appex`: an extension's `Bundle.main` is its own, so
  without that copy every complication would draw the '?' placeholder. Confirmed the PNG is inside
  the appex.

AC5 — TAP OPENS THE APP — IS STRUCTURAL ONLY, NOT OBSERVED

The entry view carries `.widgetURL(DigiVPetURL.open)` and the app registers `CFBundleURLTypes` for
the `digivpet` scheme, and `testTheComplicationTapURLUsesTheAppsRegisteredScheme` pins the two
together so they cannot drift (a widget URL on an unclaimed scheme opens nothing, silently). But NO
TAP WAS OBSERVED: `xcrun simctl openurl` is not supported on the watchOS simulator at all — it fails

alike, so the failure is the tool, not the registration. watchOS opens the containing app for a
complication tap even with no URL, so the behaviour does not depend on the scheme; the scheme is what
would let a future story deep-link. This is the one AC that was not literally verified.

REGRESSION FOUND AND FIXED IN THE SAME STORY

Adding the app-group entitlement MOVED THE SAVED GAME. `NSPersistentContainer.defaultDirectoryURL()`
returns the group container as soon as an app has app groups, so SwiftData's default store jumped to
`Shared/AppGroup/.../default.store` — seen in the test host's CoreData log the moment the entitlement
landed. On a real watch that would have orphaned an existing save and handed the user a fresh egg.
`GameStore.defaultStoreURL()` now names Application Support inside the APP's own container
explicitly, `testTheSavedGameStaysInTheAppsOwnContainer` asserts it, and the Simulator confirms
`default.store` back in the app container with only the snapshot in the group one.
