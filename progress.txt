2026-07-21 — US-095 — ActionMotion: scripted sprite motion — PASSES
================================================================================

Build green. 1171/1171 tests pass on Apple Watch Series 11 (46mm), 13 of them the
new ActionMotionTests. Nothing else in the suite changed behaviour.

WHAT WAS ADDED
- `Sources/ActionMotion.swift` — a motion TRACK, not an animator. The whole thing
  is `static func offset(for: Kind, elapsed: TimeInterval) -> CGPoint`: no timer,
  no state, no view. Five kinds — chew, hop, lunge, shake, recoil. The value type
  itself is only `kind` + `start`, so restarting a motion is assigning a new
  `start` and a spent motion just keeps answering `.zero`.
- `Sources/DigimonSpriteView.swift` — `verticalOffset`, applied in the SAME
  `.offset(x:y:)` as the existing horizontal one.
- `Sources/WanderingSpriteView.swift` — optional `motion: ActionMotion?`, ADDED
  to the walk position.
- `Tests/ActionMotionTests.swift`.

PIXELS, NOT POINTS — the reason the amplitudes live in an `Amplitude` enum
- Sprites are 16x16 drawn at a scale the caller picks (5x main screen, 4x battle,
  2x Dex). A hop written as "10 points" clears a fifth of the body at 5x and half
  of it at 2x. Written as `hopHeight = 3` PIXELS and multiplied by the view's
  scale at draw time, it is three pixels of the Digimon's own body everywhere.
- `WanderingSpriteView.motionOffset(at:)` is the ONE place that multiplies by
  `scale`. `testAmplitudesAreSpritePixels` fails any constant that drifts into
  points, by asserting no motion travels more than half a sprite.

THE PROPERTY EVERYTHING RESTS ON — zero at both ends
- Every kind returns `.zero` at `elapsed == 0` and at EVERY elapsed past its
  duration, so a view can leave a finished motion mounted forever and the Digimon
  is not stranded a few pixels off centre. `testAMotionLeftRunningStaysHome`
  walks 60s past each end; the tail is flat, not merely zero at one instant.
- Total, not just pure: negative, infinite and NaN elapsed all answer `.zero`
  rather than trapping (`testUnexpectedElapsedTimesAreAnswered`). A motion whose
  start is in the future has simply not begun.
- Shapes are built from two helpers that are zero-ended BY CONSTRUCTION rather
  than by clamping: `arcs(count:_:)` = |sin(pi*count*p)| (n humps, all on the
  same side — a plain multi-cycle sine would send the second hop as far BELOW the
  ground as the first went above) and `thrust(_:out:)` (quarter-sine out to
  exactly 1 at `out`, quarter-cosine home).

SIGNS, AND WHY LUNGE IS NEGATIVE X
- y is SwiftUI's: positive down. So `hop` is negative (up) and `chew` positive
  (the Digimon leans INTO the bowl) — deliberately opposite, and both pinned.
- x is written for an UNMIRRORED sprite, and every sheet in this pack faces LEFT.
  So `lunge` is -x (forward) and `recoil` is +x (knocked back). A caller drawing a
  mirrored sprite negates x with it — `WanderingSpriteView` does exactly that off
  `position.flipped`, or a Digimon walking right would lunge over its own
  shoulder. `shake` is the only motion that goes both ways, which is what makes a
  refusal read as a refusal.
- Peaks are found by SAMPLING the whole duration finely, not by evaluating a
  hand-derived peak time, so the tests do not re-implement the shapes they check.
  Only a changed amplitude fails them.

THE TICK — 1/30s while a motion runs, `MovementModel.step` (0.25s) otherwise
- The shortest motion is `recoil` at 0.35s. At the walk's cadence it would be
  drawn once or not at all. The faster schedule is safe for the walk because
  `MovementModel.advance(to:)` is idempotent within a step — extra samples redraw
  the same position rather than walking further.
- `testTheSamplingTickResolvesTheShortestMotion` pins both halves: at least eight
  frames for the shortest motion, and finer than the walk's step.

THE WALK AND THE MOTION CANNOT FIGHT
- The motion is ADDED to the walk offset, and it is safe to add precisely because
  US-096/097 will pass `isMoving: false` alongside a motion, so the walk is HELD
  (see `MovementModel.hold(at:)`) for the motion's whole length. Even if they did
  overlap the sprite would be somewhere sane — a walk with a bob on it.

THE ONE THING THAT COST A RUN — Date round-trips are not bit-exact
- `start.addingTimeInterval(d).timeIntervalSince(start)` is NOT `d`. A `Date` is
  absolute, so the addition rounds against a reference-date magnitude (600,000 in
  these tests) and the last bit or two of a fractional second do not survive. The
  first draft compared date-sampled and elapsed-sampled offsets bit-for-bit (49
  failures) and asserted `isRunning` false at exactly `start + duration`, which
  measures back a hair UNDER duration and so is still "running" (4 failures).
- Fixed in the TESTS, not by bending the shapes: an accuracy on the comparison,
  and the end asserted one tick past the duration. Nothing rests on that hair —
  `offset` there is already home to within a millionth of a pixel, and one extra
  `isRunning == true` costs one redraw of a sprite that has not moved. This is
  also the argument for the shapes taking an elapsed INTERVAL and only the view
  turning a `Date` into one.

PROJECT FILE
- `project.yml` needed NO edit: it globs `Sources` and `Tests` by directory, so
  both new files were picked up. Verified after `xcodegen generate` — ActionMotion
  appears in the regenerated pbxproj (8 references).

WHAT REMAINS
- Nothing on screen uses a motion yet. US-096 (eat/refuse) and US-097 (celebrate/
  lunge) are what wire `MainScreenModel` to hand `WanderingSpriteView` a motion,
  and they are also what will decide who owns the `start` date.
- `ActionMotion.isRunning` exists for a caller that wants to stop scheduling once
  a motion is spent. Nothing calls it yet — `offset` alone is enough to be
  correct, this is only enough to be cheap.

THE TWO PATHS, AND WHY THE SECOND ONE IS THE STORY

1. IMMEDIATE — this refresh landed inside the window past `notifyGrace` with the
   light not `.off`. Straight to `notifications.send(.lights, ...)`.
2. QUEUED — this refresh happened while AWAKE. Tonight's bedtime plus the grace
   is computable, so it is handed to the system there and then.

Path 2 is not a nicety. The nudge is owed at 22:10, which is close to the least
likely minute for a watch app to be running; without it a user who leaves the
light on and puts the watch down after dinner is charged US-101's mistake having
never been told anything. AC4 is right that the immediate path alone is a
feature that only works for people who happened to raise their wrist.

THE ONCE-A-NIGHT BOOKKEEPING — read this before touching US-101

`lightNotifiedNight` is stamped by BOTH paths, each with the night it is for,
and that single stamp is what makes "at most once per night" hold across the two
mechanisms. The subtle part, which cost the most thought:

- Path 2 is deliberately restricted to `windowStart(containing: now) == nil`
  (awake), and therefore only ever stamps the night that is NEXT. It never skips
  ahead. I tried the more general version first — schedule whatever the next
  nudge deadline is, from anywhere — and it is BROKEN: at 23:30, after tonight's
  nudge has gone out, it stamps TOMORROW, and then the 23:45 refresh sees
  `lastNotifiedNight (tomorrow) != night (tonight)` and fires a duplicate for a
  night already nudged. `shouldNotify` compares for INEQUALITY, so a stamp that
  runs ahead of the night being asked about reads as "not yet notified". Either
  keep scheduling to the awake case, or change `shouldNotify` to `>=` — do not
  do the first half of that without the second.
- `withdrawLightsNotice(now:)` gives the claim back only when
  `lightNotifiedNight > now`. That test is exact, not a heuristic: a stamp for a
  night whose bedtime is still ahead can ONLY have come from path 2, because
  path 1 stamps the night it is already inside. So future stamp = "queued, never
  seen" = withdraw it; past stamp = "the user has read it" = the night stays
  claimed however often the lamp is flicked afterwards. Without the withdrawal,
  a user who put the light out at 20:00 and turned it back on at 21:00 would
  never be nudged that night.
- The stamp is taken whether or not the toggle let the notice through, matching
  `claimPoopNotification`: the claim is on the NIGHT, not on the delivery.

THE ONE REAL DESIGN DECISION: EVERY UNAUDITED NIGHT, NOT JUST THE LAST

The obvious implementation — call `shouldChargeMistake` once at `now` and charge
one night — was written first, built, and FAILED `ClosedAppRecomputeTests
.testFortyEightHoursShutMatchesFortyEightHoursOpen`: 48h open scored 8 mistakes,
48h shut scored 7. The open run refreshes inside both bedtimes and charges both;
the shut run only ever sees the morning after the second. That is precisely the
asymmetry that test exists to forbid, and AC4 ("two consecutive neglected nights
charge exactly two") says nothing about anybody having been there to watch.

So `auditLights` walks back: from the most recent night whose deadline has
PASSED, it asks `shouldChargeMistake` at each night's own deadline instant, and
pays them oldest-first so `lightAuditedNight` ends on the most recent.

- Asking AT the deadline is what turns the one-night rule into a question about
  a particular night without changing the rule: at bedtime+30min,
  `mostRecentWindowStart` is that night and the grace is exactly satisfied.
- Starting at `mostRecentWindowStart` alone would be wrong at 22:10 — tonight is
  not owed yet, and last night may well be. Hence the one-step back when the
  most recent deadline is still ahead.
- Oldest-first is not cosmetic. Paid newest-first the marker would end on the
  OLDEST night charged and the next refresh would charge the newer ones again.
- The walk terminates on its own: going back, `shouldChargeMistake` fails at the
  night already audited, or at the first deadline preceding
  `lightStateChangedAt` — and every earlier deadline precedes it too, since
  `wasLit` is monotone in that direction.

Note this is the OPPOSITE choice from US-053's mess (once per spell, never a
rate) and for a consistent reason, now written into both docs and that test's
doc comment: the poop pause is observation-dependent, so a rate would score
differently depending on who was watching. Nothing about the lamp is — each
night's verdict is one timestamp against one deadline, recoverable days later.
